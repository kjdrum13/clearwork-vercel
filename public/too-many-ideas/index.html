Getting Unstuck

What to Do When You Have Too Many Ideas and Can't Pick One

Having too many ideas isn't a creativity problem — it's a clarity problem. Here's how to get unstuck and actually build something.

The notes app is full. There are three open browser tabs with half-researched business ideas. A voice memo from last Tuesday that seemed important at the time. A Notion page titled "Ideas" with 47 items, none of them developed beyond a sentence.

This isn't a lack of creativity. It's actually the opposite problem — a brain that generates ideas faster than it can evaluate them, combined with a decision-making process that treats every idea as equally worth pursuing until proven otherwise.

The result is paralysis. Not because you don't know what to do, but because you don't know which thing to do.

Why "just pick one" doesn't work

The standard advice for idea overload is to pick one and commit. And that's ultimately correct — but it skips the part that makes commitment possible: understanding why one idea is worth your time more than the others right now.

Picking randomly doesn't create commitment. It creates a situation where you're working on something you're not sure about, which makes it easy to abandon when the next idea surfaces. You need a reason to pick the one you pick.

That reason comes from honestly evaluating each idea against the same set of criteria — not vibes, not excitement level, not which one got the most likes when you mentioned it on social media.

"The problem isn't too many ideas. It's no framework for deciding which one deserves your next 90 days."

A framework for picking the right idea right now

Run each of your top 3-5 ideas through these questions. Write the answers. The one that produces the most honest, specific answers — not the most exciting ones — is usually the right one to build first.

01

Can you describe it in one sentence to a stranger?

Not a category — a specific thing. "It's basically a planning tool for people who keep starting projects and losing momentum." If you can't say it in one sentence, you're not clear enough on it yet.

02

Can you name three specific people who need it?

Not a demographic. Three actual people — or vivid, specific archetypes — whose lives would be measurably better if this existed. If you can't name them, the audience isn't defined enough.

03

What makes it different from what already exists?

Search for it. What comes up? How is what you're building meaningfully different — not just in features, but in the problem it solves or the person it serves? If the difference is thin, either sharpen it or reconsider.

04

Why are you the right person to build it?

This isn't about credentials. It's about whether you have the experience, perspective, or access that makes you more likely than a random person to build this well. If the honest answer is "no particular reason," that's worth knowing.

05

What does 90-day success look like — specifically?

One number. One thing. "10 paying customers" or "500 email subscribers" or "the first version is live and three people have used it." Vague success criteria produce vague plans. Specific criteria produce real ones.

What to do with the ideas you don't pick

The ideas you don't build right now don't disappear. They go on a list — not to be forgotten, but to be returned to when the time is right. The point of the list isn't to organize your ideas. It's to remove them from active consideration so they stop competing for your attention while you build the one thing you picked.

Every time a new idea surfaces while you're mid-build, it gets the same treatment: a quick honest evaluation and then either integrated into what you're already building, added to the someday list, or examined for whether it's actually avoidance dressed up as inspiration.

That last one is more common than most people want to admit. New ideas feel like progress. Sometimes they are. Often they're a way of not doing the harder work of finishing something.

The uncomfortable truth about idea overload

Having too many ideas and being unable to pick one is sometimes a genuine decision-making problem. But it's also sometimes a way of staying in the exciting early stage of ideas — where everything is possible and nothing has been tested — instead of doing the harder, more uncertain work of actually building.

The antidote isn't more planning. It's more honesty. About which idea you're most afraid to commit to, which one would hurt most to fail at, and which one you'd be most proud to finish. That idea is usually the right one.
