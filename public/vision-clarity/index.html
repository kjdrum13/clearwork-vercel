Vision & Clarity

How to Get Clear on What You're Building

Clarity isn't a feeling. It's a set of specific questions with honest answers. Here's how to get there before you build anything.

Most people who are stuck on a project aren't stuck because they don't know how to work. They're stuck because they were never clear enough on what they were working toward in the first place.

This shows up in a specific way: you work for a while, lose momentum, start questioning whether the idea is right, and either abandon it or restart with a slightly different version of the same thing. The cycle repeats. Not because you lack discipline — because you lack clarity.

Clarity is not the same as confidence. You don't need to be certain your idea will work. You need to be specific enough about what it is that you can make decisions, take actions, and measure whether you're moving in the right direction.

What clarity actually means

You're clear when you can answer these questions without hesitating:

What are you building, in one sentence — not a category, a specific thing? Who specifically is it for — not a demographic, a person you can picture? What problem does it solve — not the surface problem, the one underneath it? What makes it different from what already exists? Why are you the right person to build it? What are you most afraid won't work?

If any of these produce a vague answer, you're not clear yet. And that's fine — the point isn't to judge yourself. The point is to get honest about where the gaps are before you invest more time building in the wrong direction.

"Clarity is not confidence. It's specificity. You need to know exactly what you're building — not whether it will work."

The 8 questions that create clarity

These aren't new questions. But most people have never sat down and answered all of them honestly, in writing, about a single project. That act — writing honest answers to specific questions — is where clarity comes from.

  • 01
    What are you calling this thing you're building?
    Working title is fine. It doesn't need to be final.
  • 02
    In one or two sentences — what is it, exactly?
    Pretend you're texting a smart friend who's never heard of it.
  • 03
    What makes this different from everything else out there?
    Even a small difference counts. What's the thing that makes it yours?
  • 04
    Describe the person you're building this for. Be specific.
    Age, situation, how they spend their days. The more concrete, the better.
  • 05
    What keeps them up at night? What's the real problem this solves?
    Not the surface problem — the one underneath it.
  • 06
    Why are you the one to build this?
    What in your life, experience, or perspective makes you right for this?
  • 07
    What's the thing you're most afraid won't work?
    Be honest. This is the thing worth naming.
  • 08
    If this goes well, what does 90 days from now look like?
    Be specific and real — not a dream, a milestone.

What to do with your answers

Read them back. Notice where you were vague. The vague answers are where the clarity work needs to happen — not because vagueness means the idea is bad, but because vagueness means you haven't thought it through far enough yet.

The answer to question 7 — what you're most afraid won't work — is usually the most important one. It tells you where the real risk is. Addressing it directly, rather than hoping it won't be a problem, is the difference between a plan that's honest and one that's wishful.

Once you have clear answers, build your plan backward from question 8. What does 90-day success look like? What has to be true at 60 days for that to happen? At 30 days? This week?

That's a 90-day plan. Not a template someone else filled in — one built from what you actually know about what you're building.
